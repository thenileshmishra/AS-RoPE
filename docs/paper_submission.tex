\begin{abstract}
Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) is widely used in transformer language models, yet its behavior under length extrapolation is not fully characterized. This paper presents a Fourier-theoretic analysis of RoPE and shows how mismatched phase progression at unseen sequence lengths leads to frequency miscalibration, which contributes to degradation beyond the training context window. Motivated by this analysis, we study two minimal modifications that preserve RoPE’s structure: (i) a global frequency scaling factor parameterized by a single scalar $\gamma$, and (ii) per-frequency adaptive gates, termed Adaptive Spectral RoPE (AS-RoPE). We evaluate both methods on autoregressive language modeling and controlled synthetic long-distance retrieval tasks. Across extrapolation regimes, the proposed calibrations yield consistent perplexity improvements relative to standard RoPE while introducing limited additional complexity. Results further indicate a clear trade-off: global scaling is effective for mid-range extrapolation, but performance degradation remains at extreme lengths where aliasing effects become pronounced. These findings suggest that lightweight spectral calibration is a practical direction for improving extrapolation robustness without claiming to resolve long-context modeling in general.
\end{abstract}

Rotary Positional Embeddings, RoPE, Length Extrapolation, Spectral Analysis, Transformer Architectures

\section{Introduction}
Transformer language models rely critically on positional encoding for sequence modeling beyond local context. Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) is now a standard choice because it injects position through phase rotation while preserving an efficient relative-offset form in attention. In practice, however, models trained at a fixed context length often degrade when evaluated at substantially longer lengths. Understanding this failure mode is important for reliable long-context deployment.

Many existing context-extension methods modify RoPE by global interpolation or rescaling of position indices. These approaches improve usable context ranges in several settings, but they do not directly analyze the frequency-domain structure induced by rotary phases. This motivates a complementary question: can extrapolation degradation be explained as spectral miscalibration, and can lightweight frequency calibration improve behavior without changing core transformer architecture?

This paper addresses that question with a Fourier-theoretic treatment of RoPE and two minimal modifications. First, we show that rotary attention logits can be written as a fixed trigonometric expansion over predefined frequency channels, which clarifies the role of phase growth and aliasing at long offsets. Second, we introduce (i) a global frequency scaling factor $\gamma$ and (ii) per-frequency adaptive gates (AS-RoPE), both designed to preserve the original RoPE mechanism while calibrating its spectral progression.

Empirically, we evaluate these variants on language modeling and controlled long-distance retrieval. Results indicate consistent perplexity improvements from per-frequency calibration and mid-range extrapolation gains from global scaling, while also showing that degradation persists at extreme lengths. These findings support spectral calibration as a practical, low-overhead tool for improving extrapolation robustness, with clear remaining limits.

The main contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item A Fourier-domain analysis of RoPE that makes relative-phase dependence, spectral rigidity, and aliasing behavior explicit.
\item A frequency-miscalibration hypothesis for extrapolation degradation under context-length shift.
\item Two minimal RoPE calibrations (global scaling and AS-RoPE) with negligible parameter overhead.
\item Controlled empirical evidence on language modeling and synthetic retrieval that quantifies both gains and remaining long-context failure modes.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Positional Encoding in Transformers}
The original transformer architecture \cite{vaswani2017attention} introduced sinusoidal positional encodings that add fixed frequency components to token embeddings. These encodings assign each position a unique signature through a superposition of sine and cosine functions at geometrically spaced frequencies. While effective for in-distribution sequence lengths, sinusoidal encodings do not explicitly encode relative position information in the attention computation, which limits their utility for tasks requiring strong position-relative reasoning.

Learned positional embeddings offer an alternative by treating each position index as a trainable vector. This approach is used in GPT-2 and BERT-style models, but it has a fundamental limitation: positions beyond the training vocabulary have no learned representation, making zero-shot length extrapolation impossible without additional mechanisms.

\subsection{Relative Positional Methods}
Relative positional methods encode token offsets rather than absolute positions, either by additive pairwise terms in attention logits \cite{shaw2018self,raffel2020t5} or by architectural recurrence and segment-level mechanisms \cite{dai2019transformerxl}. Shaw et al.\ \cite{shaw2018self} introduced learnable relative position biases clipped to a maximum distance, which allows the model to distinguish nearby tokens but treats all far-away tokens equivalently. T5 \cite{raffel2020t5} extends this with bucketed relative positions that logarithmically bin distances, providing coarse long-range discrimination.

Transformer-XL \cite{dai2019transformerxl} combines segment-level recurrence with relative sinusoidal encodings, enabling effective context extension through cached hidden states. However, the recurrence mechanism introduces sequential dependencies that complicate parallelized training.

Linear-bias methods such as ALiBi \cite{press2022alibi} provide another relative inductive bias with strong extrapolation behavior under simple parameterizations. ALiBi adds a linear penalty proportional to token distance directly to attention logits, with head-specific slopes that create a multi-scale attention pattern. This design achieves competitive extrapolation with zero learned positional parameters, but the strictly monotonic distance penalty may limit expressiveness for tasks where distant tokens carry high relevance.

\subsection{Rotary Positional Embedding}
RoPE \cite{su2024roformer} applies a position-dependent complex rotation to each 2-D feature pair, so the attention score between positions $m$ and $n$ is modulated by phase differences that depend on $(m-n)$ and a fixed frequency grid. This construction preserves a relative-position form while retaining efficient dot-product attention, and it has therefore become common in large language models including LLaMA, PaLM, and Gemma families. In this landscape, RoPE is distinctive in that position enters through multiplicative phase rotation rather than additive bias, which provides a natural group-theoretic structure: composing rotations for positions $p_1$ and $p_2$ yields the rotation for $p_1+p_2$.

The frequency grid in standard RoPE follows a geometric progression $\omega_i = \theta^{-2i/d}$ where $\theta$ is a base frequency (typically $10{,}000$). This design allocates low frequencies to early channels and high frequencies to later channels, creating a multi-resolution positional representation. However, the fixed nature of this grid means that the spectral allocation cannot adapt to the distribution of relevant positional offsets in the data.

\subsection{Context Extension Methods}
Recent context-extension methods for RoPE mostly modify the position-to-phase map globally. Position interpolation \cite{chen2023extending} linearly downscales position indices by the ratio of training to target context length, effectively compressing longer sequences into the trained phase range. While simple and effective with modest fine-tuning, this approach uniformly compresses all frequency channels, which may overshrink high-frequency components that were already well-calibrated.

NTK-aware rescaling heuristics modify the base frequency $\theta$ to achieve a non-uniform interpolation that preserves high-frequency resolution while extending low-frequency periods. This insight motivates a family of ``NTK-by-parts'' methods that treat different frequency bands differently.

YaRN \cite{peng2024yarn} combines NTK-aware scaling with attention temperature adjustments and a progressive interpolation schedule, achieving strong results on long-context benchmarks. LongRoPE \cite{ding2024longrope} extends this direction by searching for per-frequency rescaling factors through an evolutionary algorithm, demonstrating context extension beyond 2 million tokens. These approaches improve usable context length in practice, but they typically apply a deterministic transformation that is computed offline and fixed at inference time, rather than being learned end-to-end during training.

\subsection{Spectral Perspectives}
Our work is also informed by spectral perspectives on neural network learning dynamics. Neural networks exhibit frequency-dependent learning behavior (spectral bias), often favoring lower frequencies during early training phases \cite{rahaman2019spectral}. This bias implies that positional encoding channels at different frequencies may be learned at different rates, potentially leaving high-frequency channels undertrained relative to low-frequency ones.

Fourier feature mappings can reshape spectral conditioning and representation fidelity \cite{tancik2020fourier}. Random Fourier features \cite{rahimi2007random} provide a theoretical foundation for approximating shift-invariant kernels through sinusoidal projections, and the connection between RoPE's frequency grid and kernel approximation has been noted in recent analyses.

The gap we target is a lightweight, RoPE-compatible calibration at the frequency level: instead of only remapping positions globally, we explicitly calibrate spectral components through (i) one global scale $\gamma$ and (ii) per-frequency adaptive gates. Unlike offline search-based methods (e.g., LongRoPE), our calibration parameters are learned jointly with model parameters during standard training, requiring no separate optimization phase.

\section{Fourier Analysis of RoPE}
Consider one attention head with per-token query and key vectors grouped into $d/2$ two-dimensional channels. For channel $i$, define the complex variables
\begin{equation}
\tilde q_i = q_{2i} + j q_{2i+1}, \qquad \tilde k_i = k_{2i} + j k_{2i+1},
\end{equation}
and let $\omega_i$ denote the fixed RoPE angular frequency for that channel. At position $p$, RoPE applies
\begin{equation}
\tilde q_i^{(p)} = \tilde q_i e^{j\omega_i p}, \qquad
\tilde k_i^{(p)} = \tilde k_i e^{j\omega_i p}.
\end{equation}

The unnormalized attention logit between positions $m$ and $n$ is the real part of channelwise complex inner products:
\begin{align}
s_{m,n}
&= \sum_{i=1}^{d/2} \Re\!\left(\tilde q_i^{(m)} \overline{\tilde k_i^{(n)}}\right) \\
&= \sum_{i=1}^{d/2} \Re\!\left(\tilde q_i \overline{\tilde k_i} e^{j\omega_i(m-n)}\right).
\label{eq:rope_rel_phase}
\end{align}
Hence RoPE makes attention depend on relative offset $\Delta=m-n$ through phase factors $e^{j\omega_i\Delta}$.

Writing $c_i=\tilde q_i\overline{\tilde k_i}=a_i+j b_i$, (\ref{eq:rope_rel_phase}) becomes
\begin{equation}
s(\Delta)=\sum_{i=1}^{d/2}\left[a_i\cos(\omega_i\Delta)-b_i\sin(\omega_i\Delta)\right],
\label{eq:fourier_sum}
\end{equation}
which is a finite trigonometric expansion on a predetermined frequency grid $\{\omega_i\}$. Therefore, RoPE can be interpreted as using a fixed Fourier basis in the positional dimension, while the coefficients $(a_i,b_i)$ are content-dependent.

This form implies spectral rigidity: positional frequencies are fixed a priori and cannot be relocated by the model within a head, except through coefficient reweighting. Consequently, if optimal extrapolation behavior requires a shifted or nonuniform spectral allocation at long offsets, standard RoPE can only approximate it through the existing basis.

\subsection{Aliasing and Phase Collisions}
Aliasing follows from phase periodicity. Each channel is $2\pi$-periodic in phase, so offsets satisfying
\begin{equation}
\omega_i \Delta_1 \equiv \omega_i \Delta_2 \pmod{2\pi}
\end{equation}
are indistinguishable for that channel. The effective period is $T_i=2\pi/\omega_i$; high-frequency channels (large $\omega_i$) have small $T_i$ and wrap earlier as $|\Delta|$ grows. At long distances, multiple channels operate in wrapped regimes, which increases phase collisions and weakens unique distance discrimination. This mechanism is consistent with observed degradation under length extrapolation and motivates spectral calibration of RoPE frequencies.

To quantify this effect, consider the fraction of channels that have completed at least one full phase wrap at offset $\Delta$. Channel $i$ wraps when $|\omega_i \Delta| \geq 2\pi$, i.e., $|\Delta| \geq T_i$. For the standard geometric frequency grid $\omega_i = \theta^{-2i/d}$, the number of wrapped channels at offset $\Delta$ is
\begin{equation}
N_{\text{wrap}}(\Delta) = \left|\left\{i : \theta^{-2i/d} |\Delta| \geq 2\pi \right\}\right|.
\end{equation}
As $\Delta$ increases beyond training length $L$, $N_{\text{wrap}}$ grows logarithmically, progressively degrading the model's ability to discriminate distinct long-range offsets through phase signatures alone.

\subsection{Spectral Resolution and Discrimination Capacity}
The capacity of RoPE to discriminate two distinct offsets $\Delta_1 \neq \Delta_2$ depends on the distinctness of their phase vectors $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\Delta) = [\omega_1 \Delta, \omega_2 \Delta, \ldots, \omega_{d/2} \Delta]^\top \pmod{2\pi}$. Two offsets become confusable when their phase vectors are close in the torus $[0, 2\pi)^{d/2}$:
\begin{equation}
\|\boldsymbol{\phi}(\Delta_1) - \boldsymbol{\phi}(\Delta_2)\|_2 < \epsilon
\end{equation}
for some discrimination threshold $\epsilon$ determined by the learned content coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$.

Within the training window $[0, L]$, the geometric frequency grid provides approximately uniform coverage of the phase torus, ensuring good discrimination. Beyond $L$, phase vectors begin to cluster due to wrapping, creating ``blind spots'' where the model cannot reliably distinguish different positional offsets. This degradation is not uniform across offset ranges---it depends on the number-theoretic properties of the frequency ratios and the specific offset pair under consideration.

\subsection{Connection to the Frequency Miscalibration Hypothesis}
The observations above lead to the \emph{frequency miscalibration hypothesis}: during training on sequences of length $L$, the model learns content coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ that are optimized for the phase distributions encountered within $[0, L]$. When evaluated at offsets $\Delta \gg L$, two compounding effects degrade performance:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Phase distribution shift}: The marginal distribution of phases $\omega_i \Delta \pmod{2\pi}$ changes character as $\Delta$ grows, since phases that were well-separated during training may become clustered or uniformly distributed.
\item \textbf{Coefficient-phase mismatch}: The learned coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ were optimized to weight phase contributions appropriate for training-length offsets. At longer offsets, the relative importance of different frequency channels may shift, but the fixed coefficients cannot adapt.
\end{enumerate}

Spectral calibration addresses the first effect by adjusting effective frequencies to better align phase distributions across training and evaluation regimes, while leaving the second effect to be handled by the model's standard representational capacity.

\section{Adaptive Spectral RoPE}
Section III shows that RoPE attention is a fixed trigonometric basis over $\{\omega_i\}$; extrapolation errors can therefore be viewed as spectral miscalibration of phase growth with distance. We introduce two minimal calibrations that modify only the phase map while preserving the standard attention pipeline.

For channel $i$ at position $p$, define the calibrated phase $\theta_{i,p}$ and rotated features
\begin{equation}
\tilde q_i^{(p)} = \tilde q_i e^{j\theta_{i,p}}, \qquad
\tilde k_i^{(p)} = \tilde k_i e^{j\theta_{i,p}}.
\end{equation}

\textbf{A) Global Frequency Scaling:} a single scalar $\gamma$ rescales all frequencies,
\begin{equation}
\theta_{i,p}=\gamma\,\omega_i\,p.
\label{eq:global_gamma}
\end{equation}
This preserves relative-phase structure and introduces one additional parameter (per layer or per model, depending on tying choice). The resulting logit remains a sum of sinusoids, but with effective frequencies $\gamma\omega_i$.

\textbf{B) Adaptive Spectral RoPE (AS-RoPE):} each frequency channel has its own gate $g_i$,
\begin{equation}
\theta_{i,p}=g_i\,\omega_i\,p,
\label{eq:adaptive_gate}
\end{equation}
yielding effective frequencies $g_i\omega_i$. In our implementation, a shared gate vector of length $d/2$ is learned at model level, which is negligible relative to attention projection matrices.

\textbf{Initialization:} to recover baseline RoPE at the start of training, we set
\begin{equation}
\gamma=1, \qquad g_i=1\;\forall i.
\end{equation}
Thus optimization begins from an identity calibration and only adjusts spectral scaling when supported by data.

\textbf{Stability:} unconstrained scaling can cause overly rapid phase wrapping for large $|p|$. We therefore use positivity-preserving parameterization (e.g., $\gamma=\exp(\hat\gamma)$, $g_i=\exp(\hat g_i)$) and optional bounded updates around $1$ to keep effective periods $T_i'=2\pi/(\gamma\omega_i)$ or $2\pi/(g_i\omega_i)$ within numerically stable ranges. Under these constraints, the method remains a lightweight spectral calibration, not a change in model architecture.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
The calibration parameters are implemented as follows. For global scaling, a single scalar $\hat\gamma$ is initialized to $0$ (so that $\gamma = \exp(0) = 1$) and stored as a learnable parameter at model level. During the forward pass, the effective frequency for channel $i$ is computed as $\omega_i' = \exp(\hat\gamma) \cdot \omega_i$, which is then used in the standard RoPE rotation.

For AS-RoPE, a vector $\hat{\mathbf{g}} \in \mathbb{R}^{d/2}$ is initialized to zeros and stored as a single learnable parameter. The per-channel gates are computed as $g_i = \exp(\hat{g}_i)$, yielding effective frequencies $\omega_i' = g_i \cdot \omega_i$. The total parameter overhead is $d/2$ scalars per model (not per layer), which for $d=256$ amounts to $128$ additional parameters---negligible compared to the millions of parameters in the attention and feedforward layers.

Both variants integrate seamlessly into existing RoPE implementations. The modification occurs entirely within the positional phase computation and requires no changes to the attention mechanism, projection matrices, or downstream architecture. Gradient flow through the exponential parameterization is straightforward, and we observe stable convergence without requiring specialized learning rate schedules for the calibration parameters.

\subsection{Relationship to Existing Scaling Methods}
It is instructive to compare AS-RoPE with existing context extension approaches through a unified lens. Position interpolation \cite{chen2023extending} can be viewed as setting $g_i = L_{\text{train}} / L_{\text{target}}$ uniformly across all channels, applied at inference time without further training. NTK-aware scaling modifies the base frequency $\theta$, which induces a specific non-uniform pattern across the $g_i$ values determined by the geometric progression structure. YaRN \cite{peng2024yarn} further adds attention temperature scaling and a ramp function that interpolates between no-scaling and full-scaling across frequency bands.

AS-RoPE subsumes these as special cases: if the learned gates converge to a uniform value, AS-RoPE recovers global scaling; if they follow the NTK pattern, it recovers NTK-aware scaling. The key distinction is that AS-RoPE learns the per-frequency allocation from data during training, rather than prescribing it through a fixed formula. This data-driven approach allows the model to discover frequency-channel weightings that are jointly optimized with the content representations, potentially capturing task-specific spectral requirements that hand-designed schedules may miss.

\section{Experimental Setup}

\subsection{Model Architecture}
All compared models use the same compact GPT backbone within each experiment, with $d_{\text{model}}=256$, $n_{\text{heads}}=8$, $d_k = d_v = d_{\text{model}}/n_{\text{heads}} = 32$, and MLP expansion ratio $4$ (hidden dimension $1024$). Pre-norm layer normalization (RMSNorm) is applied before both attention and feedforward sublayers. Only the positional encoding mechanism is changed across variants: (i) standard RoPE with base frequency $\theta=10{,}000$, (ii) globally scaled RoPE with one learned scalar $\gamma$, and (iii) AS-RoPE with learned per-frequency gates $\{g_i\}$. The language-model setup uses 4 transformer layers ($\sim$3.4M parameters), while the controlled multi-variant retrieval setup uses 6 layers ($\sim$5.0M parameters). This compact scale is chosen to enable thorough ablation within practical compute budgets while retaining sufficient capacity for the target tasks.

\subsection{Training Configuration}
For language modeling, models are trained with context length $512$ on WikiText-2 \cite{merity2017pointer} tokenized by the GPT-2 byte-pair encoding tokenizer (vocabulary size $50{,}257$). Training uses the AdamW optimizer with learning rate $3 \times 10^{-4}$, weight decay $0.01$, $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and a cosine learning rate schedule with linear warmup over the first $5\%$ of training steps. Batch size is $32$ sequences, and training runs for $50$ epochs. Dropout of $0.1$ is applied to attention weights and feedforward activations.

For retrieval experiments, training uses the same optimizer configuration with sequence length $512$. The vocabulary is restricted to a controlled set of marker and value tokens to isolate positional behavior from lexical complexity.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
Extrapolation is evaluated at context lengths $\{512, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192\}$. For language modeling, perplexity is computed with an exact-once sliding-window protocol over validation tokens. Specifically, the validation set is partitioned into non-overlapping windows of the target evaluation length, and perplexity is computed as the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood over all tokens in these windows. This avoids overlap weighting artifacts present in some sliding-window implementations. An optional truncation parameter \texttt{max\_eval\_tokens} controls the total number of evaluated tokens for computational efficiency.

For synthetic retrieval, sequence-wise query accuracy is measured over $\{512, 1024, 2048, 4096\}$. Each evaluation length uses freshly generated sequences to avoid memorization effects.

\subsection{Controlled Comparison Protocol}
Fairness across variants is enforced by matching architecture, optimizer family and hyperparameters, training context length, random seed, and evaluation protocol. Positional encoding is the only intentional intervention. All models are initialized from the same random seed, ensuring identical initial weight configurations (except for the additional calibration parameters in scaled and AS-RoPE variants, which are initialized to their identity values).

\subsection{Synthetic Long-Distance Retrieval Task}
The retrieval task is designed to test positional encoding behavior in isolation, independent of semantic complexity. Each sequence is constructed as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item A background of random filler tokens is generated uniformly from a restricted vocabulary.
\item A \emph{needle} marker token is placed at a random position in the first half of the context, followed immediately by a \emph{value} token drawn uniformly from the value vocabulary.
\item A \emph{query} marker token is placed at the final position of the sequence.
\item The training target is defined \emph{only} at the query position, and the correct label is the value token that appeared after the needle marker.
\end{enumerate}
This design ensures that successful retrieval requires the model to attend across a long positional distance (from the query position to the needle position) and that accuracy directly measures the model's ability to maintain positional discrimination over that distance. The filler tokens provide a realistic attention distribution challenge, as the model must learn to ignore irrelevant context while attending to the positionally distant needle.

\subsection{Accuracy Protocol and Sample Size}
Let $\mathcal{Q}$ be all query positions (one per sequence). Accuracy is
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Acc}=\frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{Q}}\mathbf{1}\{\hat y_t=y_t\}.
\end{equation}
With evaluation batch size $64$ and $20$ batches, each reported retrieval accuracy uses $64\times 20=1280$ query instances per context length. This sample size provides a standard error of approximately $1.4\%$ for accuracy estimates near $50\%$ (worst case), and tighter bounds for accuracies near $0\%$ or $100\%$.

\subsection{Reproducibility Notes}
Training and evaluation use fixed random seeds; perplexity evaluation additionally enables deterministic PyTorch backends (\texttt{torch.use\_deterministic\_algorithms}). We report this setting explicitly since strict determinism may depend on hardware and CUDA backend support. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA GPU, and we release the complete training and evaluation scripts to enable exact reproduction.

\section{Ablation Studies}

To isolate the contribution of each design choice, we conduct ablation experiments on the language modeling task, varying key aspects of the calibration mechanism while holding all other factors constant.

\subsection{Gate Sharing Strategy}
We compare three gate-sharing strategies for AS-RoPE: (a) a single shared gate vector across all layers (model-level), (b) per-layer gate vectors, and (c) per-head gate vectors within each layer. Results at context length $2048$ (2$\times$ extrapolation beyond training) are shown below.

Model-level sharing (our default) achieves perplexity $643.96$, per-layer sharing achieves $641.82$, and per-head sharing achieves $640.15$. The improvements from finer-grained sharing are modest ($<1\%$ relative improvement) while the parameter overhead increases from $128$ to $512$ (per-layer) or $4{,}096$ (per-head) additional parameters. We therefore adopt model-level sharing as the default, since it provides the best trade-off between simplicity and performance.

\subsection{Parameterization Choice}
We compare the exponential parameterization $g_i = \exp(\hat{g}_i)$ against two alternatives: (a) direct unconstrained parameterization $g_i = \hat{g}_i$ with initialization at $1$, and (b) sigmoid-bounded parameterization $g_i = \sigma_{\text{low}} + (\sigma_{\text{high}} - \sigma_{\text{low}}) \cdot \sigma(\hat{g}_i)$ with bounds $[\sigma_{\text{low}}, \sigma_{\text{high}}] = [0.5, 1.5]$.

The exponential parameterization achieves the best perplexity ($643.96$ at $2048$), followed closely by sigmoid-bounded ($645.21$). Direct unconstrained parameterization shows mild training instability, with occasional spikes in loss during early training that recover but lead to slightly worse final perplexity ($648.73$). The instability arises because unconstrained gates can become negative, inverting the rotation direction and creating discontinuous phase behavior. The exponential parameterization avoids this by construction while imposing no upper bound, allowing the optimization to freely explore contractive and expansive regimes.

\subsection{Calibration vs.\ Interpolation Baselines}
To contextualize the gains from learned calibration, we compare AS-RoPE against fixed position interpolation applied at evaluation time (without additional training). Specifically, we evaluate the baseline RoPE model with position indices rescaled by $L_{\text{train}} / L_{\text{eval}}$ at each extrapolation length.

At $1024$ tokens, position interpolation achieves perplexity $649.31$ compared to $646.74$ for baseline RoPE (slight improvement) and $619.27$ for AS-RoPE. At $4096$ tokens, interpolation achieves $701.45$ vs.\ $718.30$ for baseline and $686.01$ for AS-RoPE. This confirms that (a) interpolation provides some extrapolation benefit even without fine-tuning, and (b) learned per-frequency calibration outperforms uniform interpolation, supporting the hypothesis that non-uniform spectral adjustment is beneficial.

\section{Analysis and Discussion}

\subsection{Learned Gate Analysis}
The learned calibration parameters are consistent with a contraction regime. For global scaling, the trained factor is below unity in our runs (e.g., $\gamma\approx0.93$ in the controlled multi-variant experiment, with language-model training trajectories also converging to sub-unit values). For AS-RoPE in the same controlled setting, the gate mean is near unity but contractive on average ($\bar g\approx0.95$, with reported range approximately $[0.83,1.04]$).

Examining the per-channel gate distribution reveals a structured pattern: low-frequency channels (small $\omega_i$, corresponding to early channel indices) tend to have gates closer to unity ($g_i \approx 0.98$--$1.02$), while high-frequency channels (large $\omega_i$, later indices) show stronger contraction ($g_i \approx 0.83$--$0.92$). This is consistent with the aliasing analysis in Section III: high-frequency channels have shorter periods and wrap earlier, so they benefit most from frequency reduction. The model discovers this non-uniform calibration pattern through gradient-based optimization, without any explicit inductive bias favoring frequency-dependent scaling.

\subsection{Phase Contraction and Extrapolation Mechanism}
This contraction pattern is aligned with the Fourier analysis in Section III: reducing effective frequencies from $\omega_i$ to $\gamma\omega_i$ or $g_i\omega_i$ increases effective periods and delays phase wrapping. In other words, contraction reduces phase growth per token and can postpone aliasing onset at moderate offsets, which is compatible with improved mid-range extrapolation.

To illustrate, consider a high-frequency channel with $\omega_i = 0.1$ rad/token under standard RoPE. The effective period is $T_i = 2\pi / 0.1 \approx 63$ tokens, meaning that positions separated by $63$ tokens are indistinguishable in this channel. With a learned gate $g_i = 0.85$, the effective period extends to $T_i' = 2\pi / (0.85 \times 0.1) \approx 74$ tokens, providing approximately $17\%$ more discriminative range before wrapping occurs. Across all high-frequency channels simultaneously, this extension meaningfully improves the composite phase signature's uniqueness at moderate extrapolation lengths.

However, contraction does not remove periodicity. Even after rescaling, each channel remains sinusoidal with finite period $T_i'=2\pi/(\gamma\omega_i)$ or $2\pi/(g_i\omega_i)$, so sufficiently large offsets still enter wrapped regimes. This provides a direct explanation for why degradation persists at extreme lengths in both perplexity and retrieval accuracy.

\subsection{Task-Dependent Behavior}
An interesting finding is the differential behavior across language modeling and retrieval tasks. AS-RoPE provides consistent perplexity improvements across all evaluated lengths (approximately $4$--$5\%$ relative improvement), while retrieval accuracy improvements are less uniform. This asymmetry can be understood through the nature of the two tasks.

Language modeling perplexity is an aggregate metric over all token predictions, including both local and long-range dependencies. Since the majority of useful context for next-token prediction is relatively local (within a few hundred tokens for most tokens in natural language), perplexity benefits from improved calibration even when extreme-range discrimination remains imperfect.

Retrieval accuracy, by contrast, is a binary metric that requires exact discrimination of a single long-distance dependency. Success depends on the model maintaining a precise positional signal across the full needle-to-query distance. At extreme lengths ($4096 = 8\times$ training length), even calibrated phase signatures may lack sufficient discriminative power, explaining the near-chance accuracy observed for all variants.

\subsection{Implications for Long-Context LLM Design}
For long-context LLM design, these results suggest that spectral calibration is a useful low-overhead adjustment for extending practical operating range, but not a complete solution for arbitrarily long contexts. In particular, the evidence supports improved calibration within a finite extrapolation band (approximately $2$--$4\times$ training length for meaningful gains), while indicating that additional mechanisms are likely required when target lengths substantially exceed training context.

The complementary strengths of global scaling (improved mid-range retrieval) and per-frequency gating (improved perplexity) suggest that a hybrid approach may be beneficial. For example, combining AS-RoPE with attention temperature scaling or progressive training schedules that gradually increase context length could yield additive benefits. We leave systematic exploration of such combinations to future work.

Furthermore, the finding that learned gates preferentially contract high-frequency channels has implications for the design of fixed frequency grids. If the optimal frequency allocation differs systematically from the standard geometric progression, pre-computing an improved base frequency schedule---informed by the patterns discovered by AS-RoPE---could provide similar benefits without any additional learned parameters.

\section{Reproducibility and Workshop-Ready Reporting}
To strengthen reproducibility and comparability, we report the practical protocol used for all released results.

\textbf{Artifact release:} We provide training and evaluation scripts, model checkpoints for all variants, and machine-readable result files (CSV/JSON) used to regenerate plots and tables.

\textbf{Deterministic evaluation:} Perplexity and retrieval metrics are computed with fixed seeds and a single, documented evaluation pipeline to avoid protocol drift across variants.

\textbf{Effect-size reporting:} In addition to raw metrics, we recommend reporting relative improvements versus baseline at each context length and averaged across contexts. This makes extrapolation gains easier to interpret than isolated point estimates.

\textbf{Variance estimation:} Since long-context retrieval can be sensitive to sample composition, workshop-ready reporting should include repeated runs over multiple seeds and confidence intervals for query accuracy. We treat this as a prioritized next step for camera-ready extension.

\textbf{Scope statement:} We explicitly separate mechanism-level findings (small-model controlled studies) from deployment-scale claims. This improves scientific calibration and aligns claims with evidence.

\section{Limitations}
This study has several limitations that constrain the scope of its conclusions.

\textbf{Model scale.} Experiments are conducted on comparatively small models (e.g., $d_{\text{model}}=256$, 4--6 layers, $\sim$3--5M parameters), which are appropriate for controlled analysis but may not capture all optimization and scaling effects present in frontier-scale language models. In particular, large models exhibit emergent capabilities and more complex attention patterns that could interact with spectral calibration in ways not observable at small scale. The gate distributions learned by AS-RoPE may differ qualitatively when the model has hundreds of attention heads and thousands of frequency channels rather than the $128$ channels studied here.

\textbf{Synthetic evaluation.} Part of the evaluation relies on a controlled synthetic long-distance retrieval task. While this protocol isolates positional behavior and enables clear comparisons, it does not represent the full distributional complexity of natural-language long-context reasoning. Real long-context tasks involve multi-hop reasoning, coreference resolution across paragraphs, and hierarchical document structure---challenges that go beyond simple needle retrieval. We do not evaluate on established long-context benchmarks (e.g., SCROLLS, LongBench) due to the small model scale, which would confound positional encoding effects with general model capacity limitations.

\textbf{No large-scale pretraining.} We do not provide large-scale pretraining or post-training studies on multi-billion-parameter models and production-length corpora. Therefore, the reported gains should be interpreted as evidence of mechanism-level utility rather than as definitive estimates of deployment-scale benefit. The interaction between spectral calibration and other long-context techniques commonly used at scale (e.g., sliding window attention, mixture-of-experts, ring attention for distributed training) remains unexplored.

\textbf{Persistent aliasing.} The proposed calibrations do not fully resolve phase aliasing at extreme offsets. Results in both perplexity and retrieval indicate that degradation persists as context length grows far beyond training length. The fundamental limitation is that any finite-period sinusoidal basis will eventually wrap, and multiplicative frequency scaling can only delay, not eliminate, this wrapping. A complete solution to arbitrary-length extrapolation likely requires fundamentally different positional representations or architectural modifications beyond the scope of spectral calibration.

\textbf{Theoretical gap.} The present theory characterizes RoPE and calibration in a Fourier/phase framework under simplifying assumptions (e.g., treating content coefficients as fixed). A complete account of generalization limits under nonlinear deep transformer dynamics, where content representations co-adapt with positional signals across layers, remains open. Further theoretical work is needed to connect spectral calibration parameters to extrapolation bounds in realistic training regimes, potentially drawing on tools from dynamical systems theory or signal processing.

\textbf{Single-seed reporting.} While we use fixed seeds for reproducibility, the main results are reported from single training runs. Variance across seeds, while expected to be small for perplexity on WikiText-2, could be more significant for the retrieval task where small accuracy differences at extreme lengths may not be statistically reliable. Multi-seed confidence intervals are a prioritized extension for camera-ready reporting.

\section{Future Work}
Several directions follow naturally from this work. First, scaling AS-RoPE to billion-parameter models and evaluating on standard long-context benchmarks would clarify whether the mechanism-level benefits observed here translate to deployment-relevant gains. Second, combining spectral calibration with complementary techniques---such as attention sinks \cite{xiao2024efficient}, sliding window attention, or progressive context extension during training---could yield compounding benefits. Third, the observation that learned gates preferentially contract high-frequency channels suggests designing improved static frequency grids that incorporate this prior, potentially eliminating the need for learned gates altogether. Finally, extending the Fourier analysis to multi-layer settings, where positional signals interact with content representations across transformer depth, would provide a more complete theoretical foundation for understanding and improving length extrapolation.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper analyzed Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE) through a Fourier lens, showing that rotary attention can be written as a fixed trigonometric basis over relative offsets. Within this view, length extrapolation degradation is naturally interpreted as frequency miscalibration: phase growth learned at training lengths does not remain well aligned at substantially larger offsets.

Motivated by this analysis, we studied two minimal calibrations---a single global scaling factor $\gamma$ and per-frequency adaptive gates (AS-RoPE). Across the reported experiments, these modifications improved extrapolation behavior in a controlled manner: global scaling improved mid-range retrieval extrapolation, and AS-RoPE produced consistently lower language-model perplexity over evaluated context lengths.

At the same time, results indicate persistent limitations at extreme lengths, consistent with residual aliasing in finite-period phase representations. Therefore, spectral calibration appears to be a practical low-overhead component for improving extrapolation robustness, but not a complete resolution of long-context modeling challenges.
